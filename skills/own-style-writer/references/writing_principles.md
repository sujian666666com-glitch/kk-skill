# Writing Principles

Use these as general writing-craft advice and review criteria. They are not a fixed persona, genre, or official-document style. The user's style corpus still decides the article's voice, rhythm, and format. Apply these principles by translating them into the current genre and user request.

## Four-Part Layout

- **Title**: State the theme and core judgment accurately, briefly, and clearly. Avoid clickbait, exaggeration, vague labels, and overlong titles.
- **Opening**: Start close to the topic. Make the article's purpose, question, scene, or core judgment clear early. Avoid circling for a long time before the reader knows what the article is about.
- **Body**: Develop the argument or narrative in clear layers. Each section should carry a distinct function and move the article forward.
- **Ending**: Close cleanly. Use a conclusion, direction, recommendation, warning, encouragement, or open question that fits the article. Do not let the piece trail off.

## Bright Head, Full Body, Strong Tail

- The opening should have lift: a clear judgment, scene, question, contradiction, or hook.
- The body should have substance: facts, examples, reasoning, texture, and necessary detail.
- The ending should have force: a concise final turn, not a loose summary pasted on at the end.

## Logic And Structure

- Keep the whole article internally connected: opening, body, and ending should answer the same central question.
- Organize around the theme, split by layers, arrange paragraphs by those layers, and decide length by importance.
- If the article raises a problem, later sections should respond to it. If it gives a solution, earlier sections should prepare the need for it.
- Avoid one-point-everywhere repetition. Explain repeated material fully in one place; mention it lightly elsewhere only when needed.
- Keep concepts consistent. A term should mean the same thing throughout the article.
- Put broad conclusions at the right level. If a point could fit anywhere, it probably belongs in a higher-level section.
- Balance comprehensive coverage and focus: mention the whole field when needed, but spend the real space on the main point.

## Short, New, Real, Distinct

- **Short**: Say more with fewer words. Cut empty setup, stock phrases, repeated clauses, and paragraphs that do not change the reader's understanding.
- **New**: Give the reader a fresh angle, judgment, example, structure, or connection.
- **Real**: Use concrete facts, examples, mechanisms, scenes, and constraints. Avoid floating abstractions.
- **Distinct**: Preserve the recognizable voice learned from the style corpus. A good article should not feel interchangeable.

## Craft Checks

- **Clear stance**: The article should make it clear what it supports, questions, rejects, or recommends.
- **Precise wording**: Choose the exact noun, verb, and modifier needed. Avoid fuzzy, overbroad, or self-contradictory wording.
- **Lively language**: Use plain examples, vivid phrasing, or reader-facing explanation when it helps understanding.
- **Measured emphasis**: Do not call every point "most important." Keep proportion and surrounding context.
- **Final proofing**: Check facts, names, dates, numbers, quotes, concepts, headings, internal references, and obvious low-level errors.
